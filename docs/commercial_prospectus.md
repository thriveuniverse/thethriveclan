## 1. The Problem We Address

Institutional risk analysis fails systematically and in one direction. In every historical compound crisis examined across our two completed applications and the calibration sample of eight historical state-decline and famine episodes, contemporary institutional assessments underestimated the actual outcome by a factor of two-to-five. The bias is consistent and directional, not random. Its mechanism is structural: institutions are mandated to model specific domains — fiscal trajectory, healthcare capacity, demographic projection, financial stability, food supply — and no institution is mandated to model the interactions between them. The cumulative product of correctly modelled domain assessments aggregated additively is materially wrong about the system's actual trajectory, because the interactions that determine the trajectory are exactly what falls between the institutional mandates.

This failure mode is documented. It has been identified, in various forms, for decades. What has not previously been resolved is why it persists despite identification. Institutions presented with compound risk methodology continue, in most cases, to produce additive assessments as their standard output. Analysts trained in compound methods drift back into siloed reasoning when they return to their institutional roles. The methodology spreads slowly. The bias does not close.

The synthesis we have developed identifies why. The methodology gap is sustained by a developmental gap. The cognitive capacity required to maintain compound reasoning across domains — to hold an interaction matrix, a feedback loop, a meta-chain in working awareness while making analytical judgements under time and political pressure — is not produced by the contemporary professional environment in which institutional analysts develop. Credentialism implies the capacity without conferring the experience that produces it. Bounce culture absorbs the consequential decisions that would develop it. KPI architectures confine analyst attention to the measured perimeter of a single domain. Translation gaps prevent integrative reasoning, where it occurs, from reaching executable analytical form.

The methodology gap and the capacity gap are coupled. The institutional silos produce the developmental environment that prevents integrative capacity from forming. The absence of integrative capacity sustains the silos, because the analysts who staff them lack the cognitive resources to push against the structural arrangement that produced them. The loop is self-sustaining within the organisational structures that contain it.

Effective intervention requires breaking the loop at both points. The compound cascade methodology, applied to a specific institutional question, produces an analytical artefact that demonstrates what compound assessment yields. The decision intelligence training, applied to the institutional analyst cadre, produces the cognitive capacity to maintain compound assessment as the institutional methodological default. Neither alone is sufficient. The analytical artefact, delivered without the developmental component, becomes a one-off exercise that the institution does not internalise. The developmental component, delivered without the analytical artefact, has no anchor in the institution's actual analytical product. The combination — a compound assessment that the institution's analysts have been trained to produce themselves — is the intervention the loop's structure requires.

This is the offering we have built and that this prospectus describes.

## 2. The Offering

The combined framework has two components. They can be commissioned separately. They are most effective commissioned together.

### 2.1 The Analytical Component: Compound Cascade Risk Assessment

A compound cascade risk assessment is a structured analytical engagement that produces, for a specified institutional question, a probabilistic risk assessment that integrates across the interaction structure of the system being analysed. The engagement follows the nine-step methodology documented in the Compound Cascade Systems Modelling Framework: system boundary definition, causal chain identification, interaction matrix construction, feedback loop formalisation, meta-chain identification, scenario construction, sensitivity analysis, historical calibration, and impact conversion methodology.

Engagement deliverables comprise a master analytical model (the working document containing all chains, interactions, sensitivity analysis, and calibration), a policy brief (15–25 pages, compressed for senior decision-makers), a technical report (60–120 pages, full methodology for analysts and academic readers), and a structured presentation of findings to the commissioning institution's senior leadership and analytical staff.

The two completed applications — the Hormuz famine model and the UK structural decline model — establish the methodology's analytical product. Both applications produced findings that diverge materially from contemporary institutional consensus, and in both cases the finding is the divergence: a probability-weighted central estimate of 118–225 million excess deaths against an institutional estimate of 30–50 million at risk; a 50–70 per cent probability of accelerated UK structural decline against an institutional estimate of 10–20 per cent. The methodology's contribution is the interaction layer. The compound finding is structurally robust to disagreement about individual chain assessments and persists across the assumption-set sensitivity tests the methodology requires.

### 2.2 The Developmental Component: Decision Intelligence Training

The decision intelligence training engine is operational at https://last-prompt.com. It runs across multiple narrative contexts (skins), each presenting the practitioner with a different professional world but identical cognitive demands: the integration of partial perspectives received from domain-specific advisors against the silent testimony of an environmental state that records the cumulative consequence of every prior decision. The practitioner's reasoning is evaluated by a structured AI rubric (the Mandate) against six interdependent criteria including variable awareness, risk anticipation, multi-step planning, and temporal symbiosis (the active management of the temporal thread connecting decisions across a sequence).

For the combined offering, the relevant skin is the Compound Cascade skin currently in development, in which the practitioner occupies the role of a senior risk analyst at a coordinating institution. The skin's environmental dimensions track the analysed system's state alongside the institution's own methodological integrity. Its advisor archetypes encode the bias weights of the institutional types that produce compound cascade inputs (treasury, central bank, statistical agency, foreign affairs, health and human capital). The rubric extension operationalises temporal symbiosis explicitly as compound interaction awareness — assessing whether the practitioner's plan demonstrates consideration of how each recommended action interacts with the other chains the institution monitors.

A practitioner who completes a full run of the skin (six chapters, thirty decisions, approximately 8–15 hours of engagement) generates a Mandate Intelligence Report — a complete decision analytics package including a Command Signature radar showing performance across the six rubric criteria, a Decision Arc plotting score volatility, performance breakdowns by crisis type, and a prose Mandate Intelligence Assessment naming the practitioner's signature strength and critical gap. The downloadable journal — thirty consecutive windows into how the practitioner reasoned under pressure across an unrepeatable path through the decision environment — is the artefact that supports facilitated debrief in coaching, cohort, or longitudinal-development contexts.

### 2.3 The Combined Engagement

A combined engagement delivers the analytical component and the developmental component as an integrated programme. The institution receives the compound assessment of its commissioned question. Its analyst cadre — typically ten to thirty individuals selected by the commissioning leadership — undertakes a structured developmental programme through the engine, supported by facilitated cohort debriefs of run journals and by methodology workshops led by the principals. The combined engagement produces both the analytical artefact and the institutional capability to maintain analytical work of equivalent rigour after the engagement closes.

This is the offering's primary commercial form. It addresses both the analytical demand the buyer is consciously commissioning and the capability demand the buyer is typically not consciously commissioning but which the analysis's internalisation requires.

## 4. Market Segmentation and Buyers

The five candidate application domains identified in the synthesis paper map onto five distinct market segments, each with its own commissioning dynamics, decision-making structures, and budget cycles. The combined offering is addressable in each, but the pathway differs.

### 4.1 Sovereign Treasury and Finance Ministry

The buyer is typically a working group convened by a permanent secretary, chief economist, or chief risk officer. Sign-off requires political or quasi-political approval at deputy minister or minister level. Sales cycles are 12–24 months from first contact to commissioning. The natural entry point is a senior insider — a former Treasury official, central bank deputy, or senior multilateral economist — whose endorsement opens the working-group door. The institution's preference is for engagements that can be presented internally as methodological enhancement rather than as remediation. Contract values: £750,000 to £2 million. Plausible commissioning questions: the compound exposure of a sovereign balance sheet to climate, energy, and demographic pressures operating simultaneously; the methodology-gap audit of the institution's existing risk register; a specific compound risk assessment commissioned to inform fiscal-event preparation.

Approximate addressable institutions: 25–40 advanced-economy treasuries and finance ministries plus a further 15–25 emerging-market sovereigns with the budget and analytical sophistication to commission externally. Plausible engagement rate: 1–3 per year in establishment phase, rising to 3–6 per year at maturity.

### 4.2 Central Bank Financial Stability

The buyer is typically a research department head, financial-stability committee secretariat, or the deputy governor responsible for financial stability. Sales cycles are 9–18 months. Sophistication is high; receptiveness to methodological innovation is high. Institutional culture is more methodologically reflective than the sovereign segment, which makes the offering more readily commissionable as a research collaboration than as a procurement. Contract values: £400,000 to £1.2 million for a single-question assessment, with an additional £200,000–£600,000 for a paired developmental programme. Plausible commissioning questions: the sovereign-financial doom loop under specified rate-and-debt trajectories; the compound exposure of the banking system to climate-physical and climate-transition risks operating simultaneously; the interaction between non-bank leverage and sovereign liquidity in a defined crisis scenario.

Approximate addressable institutions: 20–30 advanced-economy and major emerging-market central banks plus the relevant supranational stability bodies (BIS, FSB, ESRB). Plausible engagement rate: 1–2 per year in establishment phase, rising to 3–5 per year at maturity.

### 4.3 Multilateral Risk Coordination

The buyer is typically a senior programme director (D-level or above in UN nomenclature, equivalent ranks at IFIs). Budget authority within pre-allocated programme budgets allows commissioning without member-state approval at lower contract levels. Sales cycles are 6–18 months. The institutional constraint is political: any framework that produces materially higher risk estimates than member-state assessments faces resistance regardless of methodological merit. The offering is most readily commissioned as an internal capability development programme rather than as a public-facing risk product. Contract values: £300,000 to £800,000. Plausible commissioning questions: cross-mandate compound risk assessment for coordinated emergency response (food, health, displacement); capability development for cross-agency coordination personnel; methodology contribution to inter-agency risk assessment processes.

Approximate addressable institutions: 15–25 major UN-system agencies, IFIs, and regional development banks. Plausible engagement rate: 1–2 per year, with significant variation around political-economy windows.

### 4.4 Corporate Enterprise Risk and Board Risk

The most heterogeneous segment. The sophisticated subset (boards of systemically important financial institutions, insurance and reinsurance risk committees, critical-infrastructure operators with explicit systemic-risk mandates) is well-budgeted and methodologically receptive. The broader subset (general Fortune 500 ERM functions, large private-sector risk committees) is methodologically conventional and requires demonstration before commissioning. Sales cycles are 3–12 months for the sophisticated subset, longer for the broader subset. Contract values: £150,000 to £500,000 for a single-question compound assessment with executive workshop; £400,000 to £1.5 million for full board-and-cadre programmes including developmental component for the firm's risk function. Plausible commissioning questions: compound assessment of the firm's strategic exposure to a specified tail scenario; risk-register methodology audit; board-level developmental programme on integrative risk reasoning for non-executive directors.

Approximate addressable institutions: 50–80 firms in the sophisticated subset within Western markets (G-SIBs, large reinsurers, integrated energy, critical infrastructure); 200–400 in the broader subset. Plausible engagement rate: 3–6 per year in establishment phase, rising to 8–15 per year at maturity, with the larger volume coming through licensed delivery rather than principal-led engagement.

### 4.5 Public Health Preparedness

The buyer is typically a national or supranational pandemic-preparedness office or a public health agency director. Mission alignment is high. Resource constraint is significant: budgets for what reads as management training are limited; preparedness funding streams are larger but require positioning the offering as a preparedness capability rather than as a management product. Sales cycles are 9–18 months and are tied to preparedness funding cycles. Contract values: £400,000 to £1 million, often grant-funded rather than commercially commissioned, which has implications for procurement form. Plausible commissioning questions: compound assessment of pandemic preparedness architecture; capability development for emergency response leadership across multidisciplinary domains; after-action analysis methodology for major outbreak response.

Approximate addressable institutions: 15–25 advanced-economy public health agencies, the WHO Health Emergencies Programme, regional health-emergency bodies (ECDC, Africa CDC). Plausible engagement rate: 1 per year in establishment phase, rising to 2–4 per year as mission-alignment compounds.

### 4.6 Cross-Segment Observation

The five segments produce different commissioning forms, different procurement structures, and different deliverable expectations. They share the underlying intervention. The implementation strategy must therefore be segment-specific from the first engagement: the framing, the entry point, the contract structure, and the pricing all vary materially by segment. A single commercial motion across all five would dilute effectiveness in each.

The principals deliver the engagement directly. The four pathways are not competitive substitutes. They are complementary:

- *Direct engagement* establishes methodological credibility through delivered case studies in the segments where direct work is required and contract values support it.
- *Licensed delivery* extends reach into segments where direct work is impractical and provides the aggregate scale that direct work cannot reach.

A plausible sequencing: direct engagement and first-licensee development run in parallel from Year 1; subscription access launches alongside the Compound Cascade skin's release in Year 1–2; embedded executive education enters first partnership conversations in Year 1 and produces material revenue from Year 3.

## 7. Competitive Position

The combined offering occupies a position that does not currently exist as a product category. Honest commercial framing requires accurate location relative to the categories it is closest to.

It is not strategy consultancy. The major strategy houses sell organisational transformation and strategic-advisory programmes. Compound cascade analysis is methodologically more rigorous than what those firms typically produce on systemic risk questions but is also narrower in scope — it answers a specific risk question rather than designing an organisational response. The relationship is complementary; strategy houses are plausible licensees rather than direct competitors.

It is not specialist risk consultancy. Specialist risk consultancies sell quantitative risk modelling, scenario analysis, and stress testing. Compound cascade analysis differs in that its analytical artefact is qualitatively probabilistic rather than quantitatively precise and that it focuses on inter-domain interaction structure rather than single-domain quantitative depth. The two methodologies are complementary; specialist risk consultancies are plausible licensees.

It is not credit ratings analysis. The major ratings agencies produce sovereign and corporate risk assessments through methodologies that are explicitly additive — they aggregate scored sub-components into composite ratings. Compound cascade analysis is a methodological critique of additive aggregation. The combined framework is therefore positioned, in the ratings landscape, as an independent compound assessment that institutional buyers commission alongside their ratings exposure rather than as a substitute for it. This positioning has commercial implications: buyers concerned about ratings agency assessments are sometimes willing to commission compound assessments as a methodological counterweight.

It is not conventional risk-leadership training. The existing market for risk-leadership training is large and crowded. The combined framework differs structurally: it does not transmit a curriculum, and its developmental claim is specific to integrative reasoning across silos rather than to risk-domain knowledge generally. Conventional risk training is not a substitute. The combined framework is more accurately a complement, addressing a specific cognitive skill that conventional programmes do not.

It is not serious-games procurement. The decision intelligence engine has narrative elements and a protagonist, and these features have repeatedly been mistaken for entertainment design. Institutional buyers should expect the engagement structures, pricing, and success criteria of professional development and analytical methodology rather than those of serious-games procurement.

The competitive position is therefore: an independent methodological capability that complements rather than substitutes for the existing risk-analysis infrastructure of its institutional buyers. This positioning is commercially advantageous in the early phase — the offering does not threaten the institution's existing arrangements — and is sustainable through the medium term as the methodology's track record builds.

The principal risk to the competitive position is methodology imitation. The compound cascade framework is documented in open working papers as a deliberate strategic choice — academic legitimisation is the precondition for institutional adoption. Open documentation creates the possibility that competing deliverers replicate the methodology without licensing it. The mitigations are three: (1) the methodology's correct application requires the developmental component, which is less easily replicated because it depends on the engine; (2) the principals' direct engagement is the credibility anchor that early buyers commission, which competing deliverers cannot replicate; and (3) the licensed-delivery channel converts plausible imitators into licensees who pay for methodology authority rather than reverse-engineering it. The strategy is to make licensing more attractive than imitation, which the structure of the offering supports.

The Year 3 range is wide — from £8 million to £22 million — because the dominant variable is licensee-channel productivity, which depends on licensee selection, methodology training quality, and the licensees' commercial commitment. The conservative case assumes 1–2 licensees of moderate productivity. The aggressive case assumes 4–5 licensees of high productivity. The central case lies between.

The principals' time ceiling on direct engagement is approximately £6 million per year at full utilisation, given current capacity. Beyond that, scaling depends on the licensee channel. The Year 3 trajectory reflects this: direct revenue plateaus while licensee revenue scales. If direct delivery is supplemented by a small full-time analytical team (2–4 senior analysts trained in the methodology under principal supervision), the direct ceiling rises to approximately £10–15 million per year, but at the cost of margin compression and significant management overhead.

### Year 4 and Beyond

A plausible mature steady-state, given the addressable market and the four-pathway architecture, is £25–60 million per year in combined revenue: £8–15 million direct, £8–25 million licensed-delivery royalties, £4–10 million subscription, £2–5 million executive education and ancillary. This range assumes continued methodology adoption across the segments and is conditional on the early case studies producing sufficient market traction. It does not assume international expansion beyond the principals' current geographic reach (UK, EU, North America, selected Asia-Pacific).

International expansion is the principal scaling option beyond the steady-state estimate. The methodology is domain-agnostic and segment-portable, but the institutional pathways vary materially by jurisdiction. Each expansion produces a multiplier on the steady-state figures above.
